### Answer these questions **Q1.** What is evaporation? **Q2.** What is condensation?

Lucas says: I got my socks wet when I was walking home in the rain. My grandad told me to hang them on a rack. The next morning they were dry.

What do you think happened?

Lucas’s socks had been wet because they had absorbed water in liquid state.

Overnight, this water had changed into gas state.

This is an example of evaporation.

Can you think of any other examples of evaporation? * Puddles get smaller over the course of the day as rainwater evaporates, changing from the liquid state to the gas state. * In the same way that puddles evaporate, wet surfaces, including floors, dry after we have cleaned them. Evaporation happens indoors and outdoors.
Why does the water level in ponds go down on dry days? * The liquid water in the pond condenses. * The liquid water in the pond freezes. * The liquid water in the pond evaporates. * The liquid water in the pond boils.
Animals, including humans, have adapted to make use of evaporation. When we are hot, we produce sweat. Sweat is small droplets of liquid water which we produce on our skin. Body heat is used to evaporate the liquid water in sweat to cool our bodies down.
Cooks use evaporation to help make tasty soups. As they heat the food in a saucepan, water evaporates leaving a thicker, stronger tasting soup.
Sweating cools us down because sweat condenses on our skin. Body heat is used to evaporate the liquid water in sweat to cool our bodies down.
Alex had been outside on a cold day. When he went back into the house, his glasses “steamed up” and he could no longer see through them.
Condensation occurs when materials in the gas state cool and change to the liquid state. For example, Alex’s glasses had become cold outside. When he returned to the house, water vapour in the warm air touched his cold glasses and condensed, forming liquid water.
Why does the cold glass in glasses “steam up” when entering a warm room? Water vapour in the air evaporates off the cold glass. Water vapour in the air condenses on the cold glass. Water vapour in the air melts on the cold glass.
When I have a hot shower or bath, the bathroom walls and windows get water droplets running down them. When we heat water in a saucepan, water condenses on the tiles behind the cooker.
I have been told that water vapour is invisible but I think I have seen it above a kettle when it boils. Water vapour is invisible. The steam you see above the top of a boiling kettle is not actually water vapour. It is tiny water droplets, formed as the water vapour cools and condenses.
Scientists learn about evaporation and condensation using observation and investigation. Ancient Greeks like Aristotle described evaporation and noted that water vapour from the sea could condense into clouds and form as rain.
